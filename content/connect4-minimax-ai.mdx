When I built the Connect 4 AI, I wanted to understand not just classical game tree search, but also how to enhance it with modern neural networks. The result: an AI that can decide its move in under 100ms—fast enough for real-time play.

## Starting with Minimax

Minimax is a decision algorithm for two-player zero-sum games. One player maximizes their score, the other minimizes it. The algorithm explores all possible future moves and picks the best one.

In Connect 4, the AI is the maximizer. You are the minimizer.

## The Problem: Speed

Pure minimax at full depth is too slow. Playing Connect 4 to completion requires exploring ~7^43 possible games. Even with aggressive pruning, this is computationally intractable.

The solution: **Alpha-Beta Pruning** + **Heuristic Evaluation**

Alpha-beta pruning cuts branches that cannot affect the final decision—roughly doubling effective search depth for the same computation time. My implementation reduced the branching factor to O(7²·⁵) instead of O(7).

## From Heuristics to Neural Networks

Here is where it got interesting. Instead of hand-crafted heuristics (piece counts, center control, etc.), I trained a **PyTorch ValueNet**: a 3-layer MLP that learned to evaluate board positions.

The network takes a 42-dimensional state vector (the board encoded as features) and predicts a win probability. This is far more flexible than rigid heuristics—the network can learn subtle positional patterns that emerge during play.

## Move Ordering: The Hidden Optimization

One trick that made a huge difference: **smart move ordering using DSA (Dynamic Search Adjustment)**.

The order in which we explore moves matters for alpha-beta pruning. If we explore good moves first, we can cut away more branches. By analyzing which moves historically lead to wins, I reordered the search to explore promising moves first.

Result: 50% fewer subtree evaluations.

## The Architecture

```
Input: 42-dim board state
Layer 1: 128 neurons (ReLU)
Layer 2: 64 neurons (ReLU)
Output: 1 neuron (Sigmoid) -> win probability
```

Simple, but effective. Training data came from thousands of self-play games.

## Performance

- Decision time: &lt;100ms on ~2,400 nodes per turn
- Search depth: 8-10 plies (moves)
- Pruning efficiency: 50% reduction in node evaluations

## What This Taught Me

This project sits at the intersection of classical AI (game trees) and modern deep learning. Minimax alone is powerful but rigid. Neural networks alone require lots of data. **The real win came from combining both**: using the structure of minimax to guide the search, and neural networks to make faster, smarter evaluations.

It is a pattern I see across AI systems: hybrid approaches often outperform single-paradigm solutions.
